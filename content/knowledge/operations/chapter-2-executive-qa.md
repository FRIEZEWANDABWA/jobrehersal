## 1. Describe your process for incident, problem, and change management.

### Perfect Answer

“I follow a disciplined ITIL-based approach because operational reliability depends on consistency, not individual heroics.

For incident management, the goal is fast restoration of service. We detect, triage, prioritize based on business impact, escalate quickly, communicate clearly, and restore operations as fast as possible.

For problem management, the focus shifts from speed to prevention. After service is restored, we perform Root Cause Analysis to identify why the issue happened and what structural changes are needed to prevent recurrence. This could involve redesigning infrastructure, strengthening monitoring, improving SOPs, or changing vendors.

For change management, the goal is controlled improvement without creating unnecessary disruption. Every major change includes impact assessment, testing, rollback planning, stakeholder communication, and approvals through governance structures like a Change Advisory Board where required.

I strongly separate urgency from recklessness. Fast action matters, but uncontrolled change creates bigger outages. Mature IT operations must be predictable, auditable, and trusted.”

## 2. How do you manage major incidents under pressure?

### Perfect Answer

“Major incidents require calm leadership, speed, and structured communication.

My first priority is protecting business continuity, so I activate a war-room approach with clear ownership. One person leads command and decision-making to avoid confusion.

I divide response into parallel workstreams—technical recovery, vendor escalation, and business communication.

We quickly assess business impact, prioritize the most critical services, activate failover where possible, and ensure leadership receives time-based updates rather than uncertainty.

Communication is critical because users can tolerate outages better than silence. I make sure affected teams understand what happened, what is being done, and what temporary workarounds exist.

After recovery, I insist on Root Cause Analysis and preventive action so the same incident does not repeat.

During a major ISP sub-sea fiber outage, this approach helped us restore over 95% of affected services within hours while protecting customer trust and executive confidence.

In major incidents, leadership is judged as much by confidence and clarity as by technical resolution.”

## 3. How do you approach Root Cause Analysis for repeated failures?

### Perfect Answer

“I approach recurring failures as a leadership issue, not just a technical issue.

If the same incident keeps returning, it usually means we are treating symptoms instead of causes.

I start by reviewing patterns—ticket history, affected systems, vendor performance, business impact, and failure frequency.

Then I use structured Root Cause Analysis, often with the 5 Whys framework, to move beyond the immediate failure.

For example, a repeated outage may appear to be an ISP problem, but deeper analysis may reveal the true root cause is single-provider dependency and lack of failover planning.

Once identified, I focus on permanent corrective action—architecture redesign, stronger monitoring, vendor replacement, process discipline, or training.

I also track repeat incident rate as a KPI because mature IT should improve over time, not keep repeating pain.

RCA is not about finding blame. It is about removing failure conditions.”

## 4. How do you minimize disruption during major IT changes?

### Perfect Answer

“My principle is simple: reliability before transformation.

Every major change starts with impact assessment—what systems are affected, what business risk exists, and what dependencies must be protected.

I strongly prefer phased rollout rather than big-bang implementation. This allows validation in smaller stages and reduces operational exposure.

Testing is critical—sandbox testing, pilot deployment, and rollback planning must exist before production changes.

Communication is equally important. Users and stakeholders must understand what is changing, when it is happening, expected impact, and fallback options.

I also ensure approvals and governance are in place for critical systems so changes are controlled, not personality-driven.

For example, during our network centralization project, we phased deployment site by site, validated performance before expansion, and maintained rollback readiness throughout. This allowed major transformation without significant operational disruption.

Change should feel like progress, not instability.”

## 5. How do you ensure service quality remains consistent across multiple sites?

### Perfect Answer

“Consistency across multiple sites comes from standardization, visibility, and governance.

First, I eliminate fragmented operations by standardizing infrastructure policies, access control models, vendor expectations, and support processes.

Second, I centralize monitoring and reporting so we have real-time visibility across all locations rather than waiting for local escalations.

Third, I use clear SLA ownership—both for internal teams and vendors—so service expectations are measurable and accountability is visible.

Regular service reviews and incident trend analysis help identify weak locations before problems become larger business issues.

For example, centralizing network architecture across sites significantly reduced recurring failures because we removed inconsistency and improved operational control.

Multi-site excellence is not achieved by solving more tickets. It is achieved by creating fewer differences.”

## 6. How do you manage change resistance from users and business teams?

### Perfect Answer

“Most resistance is not resistance to technology—it is resistance to uncertainty.

My first step is always communication. People need to understand why the change is happening, what problem it solves, and how it improves their work.

Second, I involve key stakeholders early rather than introducing change after decisions are already made. Early involvement creates ownership instead of resistance.

Third, I focus heavily on training and support. Even strong technical solutions fail if users feel abandoned during transition.

I also prioritize visible quick wins so people experience improvement early rather than only hearing promises.

For example, when implementing cloud printing, some users were initially resistant because they were used to the old receptionist-controlled model. Once they experienced faster access, stronger privacy, and less waiting time, adoption became natural.

Change succeeds when people feel supported, not forced.”

## 7. What does good IT governance look like to you?

### Perfect Answer

“Good IT governance means clear ownership, visible accountability, and decision-making that aligns technology with business priorities.

It includes structured incident reviews, SLA dashboards, monthly vendor performance reviews, change approval processes, service ownership matrices, and executive reporting that connects IT performance to business outcomes.

Governance should reduce dependency on individuals. Operations should remain reliable even when specific people are unavailable.

I also believe governance must be practical, not bureaucratic. The goal is not paperwork—it is predictability and trust.

For example, when managing multi-site operations, strong governance around vendor reviews, escalation paths, and performance dashboards significantly improved service reliability and leadership confidence.

Good governance makes IT dependable. Without it, success depends too much on personalities.”
