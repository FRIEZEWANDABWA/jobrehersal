## PART 1 — BUSINESS CONTINUITY VS DISASTER RECOVERY

This is one of the most common **senior interview traps**. You must answer it cleanly.

Many candidates confuse them. **You must not.**

### BUSINESS CONTINUITY (BCP)

**Question:** How does the **business continue operating** during disruption?

**Focus:** keep the business running.

**Examples:**

- backup internet links
- alternate work locations
- manual fallback processes
- temporary access control solutions
- emergency communication plans
- operational workarounds

This protects **business operations** during failure.

### DISASTER RECOVERY (DR)

**Question:** How do **systems and data return** after major failure?

**Focus:** restore technology capability.

**Examples:**

- backup restoration
- DR site activation
- cloud recovery
- server rebuild
- firewall replacement
- database recovery

This restores **technology capability** after failure.

**Simple executive memory:**

- **BCP = continue operations**
- **DR = recover systems**

Must be **automatic** in your interview answer.

## PART 2 — THE EXECUTIVE RESILIENCE FRAMEWORK

Use:

**PREPARE → PROTECT → RESPOND → RECOVER → LEARN**

This is one of the strongest BCP/DR leadership frames.

### STEP 1 — PREPARE

**Question:** What could **realistically** stop operations?

Risk identification comes first.

Examples: ISP failure, ransomware, power outage, access control failure, firewall collapse, data loss, vendor dependency, cyber breach, compliance shutdown.

Weak leaders plan for ideal conditions. **Strong leaders plan for failure.**

### STEP 2 — PROTECT

**Question:** What **reduces exposure** before disaster happens?

**Examples:**

- **Redundancy** — dual ISPs, backup power, clustered firewalls, replication where appropriate
- **Backups** — must be **tested**, secure, recoverable (**untested backup = fake confidence**)
- **Access control** — least privilege, identity governance
- **Vendor contingency** — what if the provider fails? alternatives
- **Communication plans** — who informs leadership, customers, vendors, operations? **Silence creates panic**

### STEP 3 — RESPOND

**Question:** When disaster happens, **how do we lead?**

**Framework:** **COMMAND → PRIORITIZE → COMMUNICATE → STABILIZE**

**A. Command**

One accountable lead — no parallel leadership confusion. Often Head of IT or designated incident commander.

**B. Prioritize**

**What must survive first?** Not all systems are equal.

Typical priority thinking:

- life safety
- customer / revenue-critical operations
- compliance-critical services
- internal convenience **last**

This is **executive judgment.**

**C. Communicate**

Most crises worsen with poor communication.

Communicate: what happened, impact, actions, timelines, temporary workarounds. **Leadership confidence matters.**

**D. Stabilize**

Contain damage, protect continuity, prevent escalation — failover, temporary routing, backup access models.

### STEP 4 — RECOVER

**Question:** How do we restore **full** service?

**Framework:** **Restore → Validate → Secure → Resume**

Recovery must be **safe**, not rushed. Restoring compromised systems without validation can create a **bigger** disaster.

### STEP 5 — LEARN

**Question:** How do we get **stronger** next time?

After-action review: what failed, what worked, what must improve, what investment is now justified.

**Crises should improve maturity** — not repeat the same pain.

## PART 3 — RTO VS RPO (MUST KNOW)

Extremely common interview question.

**RTO — recovery time objective**

**Question:** How **fast** must service return?

Example: email must recover within two hours → **time target.**

**RPO — recovery point objective**

**Question:** How much **data** can we afford to lose?

Example: maximum acceptable loss = fifteen minutes → **data loss tolerance.**

**Simple memory:** **RTO = time.** **RPO = data.**

Answer **instantly** and tie to **business** impact.

## PART 4 — BACKUP STRATEGY FRAMEWORK

**3-2-1 rule** (know it cold):

- **3** copies of data — original + two backups
- **2** different storage types — e.g. disk + cloud, local + external
- **1** offsite copy — fire, ransomware, physical disaster

**Critical rule:** **Backup is useless if restore is untested.** Testing matters more than backup reports. That is executive maturity.

## PART 5 — CRISIS LEADERSHIP

This is not only technical — **it is leadership.**

During crisis:

- teams borrow your **calm**
- executives judge your **confidence**
- customers judge **trustworthiness**

**Strong crisis leaders:** stay calm, decide fast, communicate clearly, remove confusion, protect trust.

**Weak leaders:** panic, delay escalation, hide information, lose credibility.

This matters massively.

## PART 6 — HOW TO JUSTIFY RESILIENCE INVESTMENT

Most resilience spending is hard to approve because success looks like **“nothing happened.”**

Use:

**RISK → EXPOSURE → COST OF FAILURE → INVESTMENT JUSTIFICATION**

**Weak:** “We need better DR.”

**Strong:** “Our current recovery posture creates unacceptable operational and compliance risk. A major outage would stop operations and create significant financial exposure.”

Executives approve **risk reduction** — not vague fear.

## PART 7 — STORY ANCHORS (BCP / DR / CRISIS)

Use real episodes as **STAR-ready** proof:

- **Sub-sea fiber / ISP failure** — BCP, failover, crisis leadership, vendor escalation.
- **Core switch failure (e.g. remote site)** — remote recovery, incident command, restoration discipline.
- **Firewall failure across sites** — urgent executive decisions, resilience investment narrative.
- **Major customer / platform escalation** — protecting trust while operations are under stress.

Prepare each with: **command structure → prioritization → communication cadence → technical recovery → validation → learning loop.**

## PART 8 — EXECUTIVE PRINCIPLE

Never answer: **“I recover systems.”**

Answer: **“I protect business continuity and trust during operational failure.”**

That is Head of IT language.
