## SECTION 7 — EXECUTIVE BEHAVIOURAL & PRESSURE QUESTIONS

### CHAPTER 1 — Perfect executive behavioural questions & answers

This is the section that often decides the final result.

Because panels ask themselves:

**Can we trust this person when pressure becomes real?**

Not: “Are they technically strong?”

At senior level, trust is built through **ownership, calm under pressure, decision-making maturity, leadership presence, accountability, and executive judgment.**

These answers must sound like **predictable leadership under pressure** — not lucky stories.

## 1. Describe a time IT services failed and how you handled it.

### Perfect Answer

“One major example was when our main ISP suffered a sub-sea fiber cut that disrupted VPN-based services across multiple sites, affecting access control, VoIP, and several client operations.

This created immediate operational risk because both internal operations and customer-facing services were affected.

My first priority was restoring business continuity, not just troubleshooting.

I immediately activated a war-room approach with clear ownership. One team coordinated directly with the ISP and backup providers, another handled failover routing and temporary connectivity solutions, while another focused on client communication and leadership updates.

We prioritized customer-critical services first and requested temporary bandwidth upgrades to stabilize backup links.

At the same time, I ensured communication remained proactive because during outages, silence damages trust faster than downtime itself.

Within six hours, we restored over 95% of services with minimal customer disruption.

The biggest lesson was that incident leadership is not only about technical recovery—it is about protecting confidence, operational continuity, and executive trust.”

## 2. Describe a time you inherited a weak-performing team or system.

### Perfect Answer

“One strong example was when I inherited a fragmented multi-site network environment where each location operated independently with separate firewalls, ISPs, support models, and inconsistent standards.

This created recurring outages, poor visibility, weak accountability, and a high volume of repeated support issues.

Instead of treating it as isolated technical problems, I approached it as a system and leadership issue.

I started by assessing incident trends, vendor performance, team ownership gaps, and the financial impact of recurring downtime.

It became clear that the real problem was lack of standardization and weak operational governance.

I built a phased transformation plan focused on centralization, visibility, vendor consolidation, and stronger ownership structures.

This significantly reduced network-related incidents by over 80%, improved customer satisfaction, and created major cost savings.

The key lesson was that weak performance often comes from weak systems, not weak people.”

## 3. Describe a time you delivered results with limited resources.

### Perfect Answer

“One strong example was improving resilience during repeated connectivity instability without immediate access to full infrastructure replacement budget.

The business needed stronger uptime, but full redesign could not happen immediately.

Instead of waiting for ideal funding, I prioritized the highest-risk exposure first.

I focused on failover readiness, stronger monitoring, vendor renegotiation, temporary redundancy improvements, and optimization of existing infrastructure.

We improved visibility, reduced avoidable downtime, and strengthened operational continuity while building the business case for longer-term investment.

This approach protected customer operations immediately while giving leadership confidence to approve future strategic improvements.

The lesson was that resource limitations should drive smarter sequencing, not leadership paralysis.”

## 4. Tell us about a time you led a team through pressure or crisis.

### Perfect Answer

“One major example was during a critical firewall failure affecting multiple active business locations.

This created immediate operational risk because access control, internet stability, and customer operations were all exposed.

My first step was creating control. I established clear ownership across the team—technical recovery, vendor escalation, temporary continuity measures, and executive communication.

I made sure priorities were clear so the team focused on business-critical restoration first rather than trying to solve everything at once.

At the same time, I maintained calm and visible communication because teams borrow confidence from leadership during crisis.

We stabilized operations quickly, secured urgent replacement approvals, and avoided major customer disruption.

The key lesson was that pressure leadership is not about doing everything yourself—it is about creating confidence, clarity, and fast execution through the team.”

## 5. Describe a time you handled a poor performer.

### Perfect Answer

“I once had a junior technician who was underperforming and lacked confidence. Initially, it looked like a capability issue, but after spending time with him, I realized the real issue was unclear ownership and low confidence rather than lack of skill.

I started by setting clearer expectations and giving him responsibility beyond routine support work.

I paired him with me on larger projects and gradually gave him ownership of meaningful deliverables instead of only assigning support tasks.

Eventually, I trusted him to lead the POS system build for a new center.

That responsibility changed his confidence significantly, and he delivered successfully.

Today, he operates much more independently and with stronger ownership.

That experience reinforced that leadership is not only about correcting weakness—it is about identifying potential and creating the environment for it to grow.”

## 6. Why do you want to leave your current role?

### Perfect Answer

“I have had strong opportunities to deliver meaningful transformation—improving infrastructure resilience, leading multi-site operations, driving cost optimization, and building stronger service delivery.

What I am looking for now is broader strategic scope and larger leadership impact.

I want to operate in an environment where IT is deeply connected to business strategy at scale—where decisions around resilience, customer experience, security, and operational excellence directly shape business growth.

I am motivated by complexity, transformation, and long-term value creation.

This next step is not about leaving something negative—it is about moving toward a larger leadership platform where I can contribute at a higher level.”

## 7. Why should we choose you?

### Perfect Answer

“You should choose me because I combine operational depth with executive leadership discipline.

I have led complex multi-site environments supporting critical business operations, with strong focus on uptime, resilience, vendor control, financial discipline, and customer trust.

I do not approach IT as infrastructure alone—I approach it as business continuity, risk management, and measurable value creation.

I have successfully delivered major transformation initiatives including centralized network modernization, service optimization, vendor consolidation, and customer experience improvements with measurable financial impact.

I also lead with calm, structure, and accountability. Under pressure, I focus on clarity and decision-making rather than noise.

Most importantly, I align technology with business outcomes, which is what senior IT leadership ultimately requires.

I believe that combination makes me a strong fit for executive-level IT leadership.”

## Executive rule for behavioural interviews

Never answer: **“This happened to me.”**

Answer: **“This is how I lead when it happens.”**

That is what gets senior roles.
