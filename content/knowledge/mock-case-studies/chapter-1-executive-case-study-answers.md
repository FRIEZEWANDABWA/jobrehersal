### CASE STUDY 1

**“A ransomware attack hits your company tomorrow morning. What do you do?”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“My leadership approach would begin with containment and business continuity, not technical investigation.

First, I would immediately activate incident response ownership with one clear command structure so there is no confusion between IT, Security, vendors, and executive leadership.

The first technical priority would be isolating affected systems, disabling compromised access, restricting privileged movement, and preventing lateral spread.

At the same time, I would identify critical business operations—customer platforms, finance systems, logistics operations, access control—and prioritize continuity for the services the business cannot afford to lose.

Executive communication would happen immediately. The CEO, leadership team, legal, compliance, and where necessary regulators must understand both the exposure and the decisions required.

Recovery would only happen through validated restoration, not rushed system recovery. Untested restoration creates repeat failure.

After stabilization, I would drive Root Cause Analysis—identity compromise, privileged access gaps, vendor exposure, or backup weakness—and ensure the operating model becomes stronger.

Cyber incidents are trust events. My role is to protect operations, confidence, and recovery discipline simultaneously.”

### CASE STUDY 2

**“The CEO says reduce IT costs by 30% immediately.”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“My first response would be to protect business continuity before reducing cost.

A 30% reduction can be dangerous if done emotionally, so I would begin by separating critical operational spend from optimization opportunities.

I would review vendors, license usage, duplicated platforms, underused subscriptions, support inefficiencies, and technical debt costs that are creating hidden financial waste.

The goal is optimization before reduction.

For example, vendor consolidation, license cleanup, cloud rightsizing, and stronger procurement discipline often create major savings without damaging resilience.

I would avoid cutting redundancy, cybersecurity controls, or recovery capability without understanding the operational risk because cost savings that create business failure are false savings.

I would present phased options with business impact visibility so leadership can choose controlled reduction rather than blind cuts.

Cost discipline should strengthen the business, not weaken its ability to operate.”

### CASE STUDY 3

**“You inherit a failing IT department. What happens in your first 30 days?”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“My first 30 days would focus on listening, stabilization, and trust—not immediate restructuring.

First, I would assess service health, recurring incidents, major operational pain points, stakeholder confidence, vendor performance, and team morale.

I would meet leadership, business units, and the IT team to understand both technical reality and political reality because inherited environments fail for both reasons.

Second, I would stabilize critical services first—anything affecting customer operations, revenue, compliance, or trust cannot wait for long-term strategy.

Third, I would identify quick visible wins—removing a recurring outage, improving escalation discipline, restoring vendor accountability—because early confidence matters.

Only after understanding people, risk, and operational exposure would I move into structural redesign.

Leadership credibility is built faster through visible control than through immediate reorganization.”

### CASE STUDY 4

**“The board says your disaster recovery budget is too high. Defend it.”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“I would frame disaster recovery as continuity insurance, not infrastructure spending.

The real question is not how much DR costs—it is how much business failure costs when recovery is weak.

I would present the financial and reputational exposure of downtime: customer disruption, SLA penalties, revenue loss, compliance consequences, and trust damage.

For critical operations, even a few hours of downtime can exceed the annual DR investment required to prevent that risk.

I would also show recovery confidence through tested restoration, backup validation, and operational dependency mapping—not theoretical preparedness.

Executives fund protection faster when they understand the cost of inaction.

DR is not a technical luxury. It is a business survival decision.”

---

### CASE STUDY 5

**“Global leadership wants standardization, but local teams strongly resist.”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“My approach would be controlled alignment, not forced compliance.

First, I would separate what must be standardized—cybersecurity controls, identity governance, reporting discipline, DR expectations—from what can remain locally flexible such as vendor realities, operational workflows, and market-specific execution.

Second, I would understand the real reason for resistance. Often it is not resistance to standards, but concern that central decisions ignore local operational reality.

I would use evidence—service reliability, customer impact, compliance exposure—to evaluate whether local resistance is valid or simply comfort with old ways.

Where local exceptions are justified, I would support structured exceptions with visibility and accountability rather than hidden workarounds.

Regional leadership succeeds when standards create trust, not resentment.”

---

### CASE STUDY 6

**“A major vendor fails during peak business operations. What do you do?”**

**Perfect executive answer**

“My first priority would be protecting continuity, not assigning blame.

I would activate incident ownership immediately and establish clear communication between internal teams, vendor leadership, and executive stakeholders.

The focus would be service continuity—temporary workarounds, failover options, customer communication, and protecting critical operations first.

At the same time, I would escalate firmly with the vendor using SLA expectations, executive escalation paths, and recovery timelines.

Leadership needs visibility quickly—what is affected, how long exposure may last, and what decisions are required.

After stabilization, I would review vendor dependency risk because a failure severe enough to threaten operations often reveals deeper strategic weakness.

Vendor failure should trigger stronger resilience, not only incident closure.”
