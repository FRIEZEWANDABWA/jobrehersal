## SECTION 5 — PEOPLE LEADERSHIP & CULTURE

### CHAPTER 1 — EXECUTIVE PEOPLE LEADERSHIP, TEAM PERFORMANCE & CULTURE BUILDING

**Perfect executive questions & answers**

This is where senior panels decide:

**Can this person lead people—not just technology?**

Because at Head of IT level, success is not about how much you personally know.

It is about:

- how your team performs
- how you handle pressure
- how you build trust
- how you create accountability
- how you influence across the business

These answers must sound like **leadership systems**—not personality descriptions.

## 1. Describe your leadership style.

### Perfect Answer

“My leadership style is structured, calm, and accountability-driven.

I believe strong teams perform best when expectations are clear, ownership is visible, and people feel trusted to deliver.

I lead with clarity first—clear responsibilities, defined SLAs, and strong communication—because many performance issues come from confusion, not capability.

I also believe strongly in empowerment. I delegate outcomes, not just tasks, because ownership creates growth and stronger future leaders.

At the same time, I maintain high accountability. Support and trust are important, but commitments must remain measurable and visible.

Under pressure, I focus on stability and confidence. Teams borrow calm from leadership, so consistency matters more than intensity.

My goal is not to be the person everyone depends on. It is to build teams and systems that perform reliably even when I am not in the room.”

## 2. How do you motivate IT teams under pressure?

### Perfect Answer

“I focus on clarity, stability, and visible progress.

Pressure becomes dangerous when teams feel uncertainty, not just workload.

My first step is to simplify priorities—what matters most right now, what can wait, and who owns what. Removing confusion immediately improves confidence.

Second, I maintain calm and transparency. Teams need leadership that creates control, not panic.

Third, I break large problems into manageable wins and celebrate progress, even small wins. Momentum protects morale.

I also stay close to blockers—whether that is vendor delays, unclear decisions, or resource constraints—because support is part of motivation.

During our multi-site network unification project, pressure was high because operations could not tolerate failure. Breaking execution into phases, assigning ownership clearly, and recognizing progress helped the team stay focused and confident.

Motivation under pressure is less about speeches and more about creating stability.”

## 3. Tell us about a time you led a team through pressure or crisis.

### Perfect Answer

“One strong example was during a major ISP sub-sea fiber outage that disrupted VPN-based services across multiple sites, affecting access control, VoIP, and several client operations.

The environment was highly pressured because customer-facing services were impacted and leadership needed immediate confidence.

I immediately activated a war-room structure with clear ownership. One team focused on ISP escalation and failover routing, another handled backup connectivity and temporary service restoration, while another supported communication to leadership and affected clients.

My focus was not only technical recovery, but team stability. I kept priorities clear, removed confusion, and ensured decisions moved quickly.

Within hours, we restored over 95% of affected services and protected customer trust through proactive communication.

The key lesson was that leadership during crisis is not about doing everything yourself—it is about creating control, confidence, and fast execution through the team.”

## 4. Tell us about a time you influenced business decisions using data.

### Perfect Answer

“One strong example was during recurring network instability across multiple centers.

Initially, these were treated as isolated technical complaints, but I believed the issue was larger and affecting customer experience and revenue.

I conducted a structured review of incident trends, downtime patterns, support ticket volumes, customer escalations, and operational losses caused by outages.

The data showed that over 60% of support tickets were network-related and recurring failures were contributing to customer dissatisfaction and event cancellations.

I presented this to management not as a technical issue, but as a business continuity and financial risk.

Using that analysis, I recommended centralized network transformation with unified monitoring, vendor consolidation, and failover design.

Because the discussion was framed around business exposure rather than technical preference, leadership approved quickly.

That project later reduced incidents by over 80% and created significant cost savings.

Data becomes powerful when it changes decisions, not just reports activity.”

## 5. Describe a time you handled a poor performer.

### Perfect Answer

“I believe poor performance should first be understood before it is judged.

In one case, I had a junior technician who was underperforming and lacked confidence. Instead of assuming lack of capability, I looked deeper and realized the issue was confidence and unclear ownership rather than skill.

I started by setting clearer expectations and giving him controlled responsibility rather than only support tasks. I paired him with me on larger projects so he could learn through real exposure.

As trust grew, I challenged him to take ownership of building the POS system for a new center.

That responsibility changed his confidence significantly. Today, the system is being deployed successfully and he has grown into a much stronger independent engineer.

Leadership is not only correcting weakness—it is unlocking potential where others may only see performance gaps.”

## 6. Describe a time you implemented a difficult change.

### Perfect Answer

“One major example was implementing cloud printing across our centers.

Previously, all printing was controlled manually through reception, which created congestion, delays, confidentiality concerns, and poor customer experience.

The challenge was that people were comfortable with the old process, even though it was inefficient.

I started by focusing on the why—showing how the current model was slowing operations, frustrating clients, and limiting privacy.

I involved key stakeholders early, designed a system with secure PIN-based release and individual accounts, and ensured training and support during rollout.

Instead of forcing adoption, I focused on visible quick wins—faster access, less waiting time, and stronger confidentiality.

The result was zero congestion, better user experience, and over 6 million KES per month in new printing revenue.

Difficult change succeeds when people experience improvement, not just hear about it.”

## 7. How do you promote continuous learning in your team?

### Perfect Answer

“I believe strong teams are built through continuous capability growth, not just daily execution.

I promote learning by combining formal development with real ownership.

First, I create stretch opportunities—giving team members responsibility for projects that challenge them beyond routine support work.

Second, I use mentorship and pairing on major initiatives so learning happens in real operational environments, not only in theory.

Third, I encourage knowledge sharing across the team so expertise does not remain with one person. This reduces dependency risk and improves resilience.

I also support structured certification and technical development where it aligns with business needs.

For example, one junior team member grew significantly after being trusted to own the POS system build for a new center. Responsibility often teaches faster than training alone.

Continuous learning is not a side activity—it is part of operational strength.”

## Executive rule for people leadership interviews

Never answer: **“I manage people.”**

Answer: **“I create clarity, ownership, accountability, and future leaders.”**

That is how senior leaders sound.
